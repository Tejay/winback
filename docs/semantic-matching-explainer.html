Semantic Changelog Matching

An interactive walkthrough of why ILIKE substring matching breaks, how an LLM re-rank fixes it, what the recovery funnel actually looks like (spoiler: subscribers don't come back from the email itself), and what else should change once we move from "matching letters" to "matching meaning." Today: ILIKE compares letters, not meaning

When the founder posts a changelog, we run changelog_text ILIKE '%' || trigger_keyword || '%' against every subscriber. It only matches if the literal substring appears. Click each subscriber to see what ILIKE decides.

The fundamental problem: ILIKE compares letters. Recovery requires comparing meaning.

New: LLM re-rank reads meaning

Replace the substring filter with one Claude Haiku call that reads the changelog and judges each subscriber's stated need. Click each subscriber to see what the LLM decides — and why.

Subscribers don't come back from the email itself

It's tempting to think "we send an amazing email and they come back." Reality is more honest: emails are conversation hooks. Recovery happens through replies and clicks. Click each stage to see what the email is actually trying to do.

The actual recovery funnel
1
First email — opens the conversation
"What specifically went wrong? Hit reply — one line is enough."
The first email's job is not to convert. It's to prove a human is paying attention so the subscriber feels comfortable replying. Look at the actual classifier prompt rule:
"For Tier 2 and Tier 3, always end firstMessage.body with a single genuine question asking why they left. Frame it as curiosity, not a survey."
A small percentage of recipients reply. That reply is the goldmine — real signal about why they actually left.
↓
2
Reply triggers re-classification
"Read their reply carefully — it is the highest-signal input."
The reply is sent back into the classifier. Now the AI knows specifically what's blocking them — not from Stripe enums and tenure days, but from the subscriber's own words. The follow-up email responds to those words directly.
Reply: "honestly the price felt high for what I was using"
Follow-up: "Totally fair — most people on the Pro plan use it heavily. The Starter plan at $9/mo might fit better. Want me to switch you over?"
This is where most of the actual recoveries happen.
↓
3
For non-repliers: changelog match → win-back email
Months later, when something they wanted ships
For subscribers who didn't reply but mentioned a specific concern, we wait. When the founder ships something matching that concern, we send a win-back email. Same goal as email #1 — open a conversation or get a click. Most won't engage. A small percentage will reply ("oh interesting, how does it handle X?") or click the reactivation link.
This is where today's winBackBody lives. And where ILIKE → LLM re-rank improves the matching, and where "concrete vs generic" improves the email content.
↓
4
Click → reactivation link → checkout
The actual recovery action
Every email ends with a reactivation link. Clicking it opens Stripe Checkout (for fully cancelled subscribers) or resumes a still-active subscription. This is the moment of recovery — the email's job was just to get them to this link.
Tracked as billingPortalClickedAt on the subscriber row. Strong attribution signal.
↓
5
90-day backstop
One last attempt for everyone else
Subscribers who never replied, never clicked, and never matched a changelog get one final re-engagement email at 90 days. Tiny conversion rate, but cheap to send and a small slice do come back if context has changed. After this, we accept they're gone.
Key insight: Every email is a hook to start a conversation or trigger a click. Subscribers rarely come back because an email was eloquent — they come back because we surfaced the small percentage who were ever going to reconsider, and made it easy for them to take the next step.
This is why "concrete vs generic" matters in pillar 3 of the next tab. Concrete emails generate more replies and clicks. They don't magically convert.

If matching changes — should the rest of the pipeline change too?

Three things were designed together, for the ILIKE world. Two of them might no longer fit. Click each pillar to see why.

1
Matching
ILIKE → LLM re-rank
This is the change we're proposing. Read the first 3 tabs above.
SQL ILIKE → 1 Haiku call returning JSON of true/false per subscriber.
2
What we store
trigger_keyword → trigger_need
Why it should also change: A single short keyword exists only because ILIKE needs literal text. With an LLM, we can store a richer description and match more accurately.
Before: "csv export"
After: "Wants to export their data to a spreadsheet for their accountant"
With the richer description, the LLM has more signal — Carol's "zapier" need could be stored as "wants workflow automation via Zapier or similar tools" and would correctly match a Zapier-adjacent feature.
3
When we write the email
generic template → concrete & specific
Why it should also change: Today's winBackBody is written at cancellation time, before any fix has shipped. The classifier doesn't know how the founder will fix the issue, so it writes a vague template ("we made improvements"). Generic emails open fewer conversations than concrete ones with real specifics.
Pre-written at churn (generic):
"Hi Bob, you mentioned CSV export. We've made improvements. Come back?"

Generated at match (concrete):
"Hi Bob, you mentioned needing CSV export. We just shipped spreadsheet downloads — here's a 30-second walkthrough of how it works for your data..."
The point isn't that the second email "converts" better in some magical way. It's that specific, concrete emails get more replies and clicks than vague templates — and replies/clicks are the real recovery mechanism (see "How recovery actually works" tab).

The shippable sequence

Each piece is independently shippable:
• Spec 19a — LLM re-rank (1) → catches semantic matches today, no schema change
• Spec 19b — Rich need descriptions (1+2) → higher match quality, small schema rename
• Spec 19c — Generate at match time (1+2+3) → accurate, specific emails — full value

Bottom line

Spec 19a alone adds ~$0.001 per changelog post. A founder posts changelogs maybe weekly. Negligible.

Full design (19c) shifts cost from "always" (every churn pre-writes a body) to "only when needed" (every match generates a body). Net cost is roughly flat — and matches happen far less often than churns, so the full design is actually cheaper per recovery.
